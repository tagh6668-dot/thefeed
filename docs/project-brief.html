Project Brief

TheFeed

News and messaging over DNS — when everything else is taken away

June 2026  ·  Open-source  ·  github.com/sartoopjj/thefeed

1. The Problem

When governments shut down the internet during protests, millions of people are cut off from the outside world overnight. They lose access to independent news, to their families abroad, and to any way of knowing what is actually happening around them.

TheFeed addresses both sides of this problem: it delivers independent news through DNS queries — the most basic and hardest-to-block layer of the internet — and includes an encrypted messenger that allows people to send short text messages to contacts outside the country, even during a total shutdown.

During these shutdowns, the tools people normally rely on stop working. VPNs become unaffordable — some sold for as much as $6 per gigabyte. Satellite internet carries severe legal risk: possession alone can lead to espionage charges. Even those who can afford circumvention tools face the constant threat of confiscation and prosecution.

Meanwhile, authorities maintain their own connectivity. Government-aligned operatives receive unrestricted SIM cards to spread propaganda, while ordinary people are left in an information vacuum — unable to verify rumors, contact loved ones, or document what they witness.

The existing censorship-circumvention tools all share a fundamental limitation: they try to establish a full TCP tunnel through the firewall. During a severe shutdown, this approach fails. DNS resolvers slow to a crawl, packet loss exceeds 80%, and establishing a reliable connection becomes nearly impossible. Tools like DNSTT, designed for DNS tunneling, require fast resolvers that take days of scanning to find — time people don't have.

2. How TheFeed Was Born

On Thursday, January 8, 2026 (18 Dey 1404), on the twelfth day of nationwide protests, the government shut down the internet. NetBlocks confirmed the total blackout at approximately 8:30 AM local time. Restrictions partially eased on January 28, but on February 28 — when the war began — the internet was cut completely again. This second phase lasted 88 days, until May 26. Combined, the disruption spanned nearly five months and became the longest nationwide internet shutdown ever recorded in any country, affecting 92 million people.

I had purchased expensive circumvention tools and managed to keep a fragile connection alive through VPNs. But after weeks of instability, my last VPN went dead. I had no access to free news. The only remaining option was television — which was jammed with interference and propaganda. I didn't even own a TV.

I kept thinking: what if there was a protocol that could receive data gradually through DNS — not by trying to open a full tunnel, but by pulling content fragment by fragment? That would be fundamentally faster than tools like DNSTT, because it wouldn't need a persistent connection at all. It just needed to move text, piece by piece.

For several days, while my VPN was down, I refined the idea in my head. When my connection finally came back, I started building it immediately — with the help of AI coding tools, I worked until 4 in the morning. By dawn, I had a working prototype running on a server I owned abroad.

That same night, I shared TheFeed on GitHub and posted about it in several Telegram groups and under the comments of major channels. Then I went to sleep.

When I woke up, my VPN was dead again. This time it stayed down for three days. But something was different: I still had access to the news. My own tool was keeping me connected. The suffocating feeling of being a prisoner in an information blackout — it was gone.

And when I checked back online three days later, I found that people were already sharing TheFeed across their own channels.

3. What I Built

From that first night onward, I worked on TheFeed around the clock — often 10 or more hours a day, with some sessions stretching past 20 hours — while simultaneously holding down an 8-hour day job. I would code during work breaks and through the nights, trying to sleep a few hours when I could. Speed mattered: I never knew when my VPN would go down again, so every window of connectivity was used to push updates.

Key milestones — all built and shipped while the shutdown was still ongoing:

  • Core DNS protocol — A feed reader that pulls Telegram and X (Twitter) content through standard DNS queries. No tunnel, no persistent connection. Content arrives fragment by fragment and assembles on the device.
  • Android app — A native mobile client released 6 days after the first prototype, so people could read news on their phones without needing a terminal emulator.
  • Public configurations — Pre-configured server profiles so users without their own server (or the money to rent one) could still connect and read news through community-run infrastructure.
  • Media downloads via DNS and GitHub — Photos, videos, and files delivered through DNS queries and GitHub's CDN, so users could watch speeches, view evidence, and even download app updates from within TheFeed itself.
  • TeleMirror (Google relay) — A second, independent content path using Google's services as a proxy, allowing users to follow any Telegram channel — not just the ones pre-configured by server operators.
  • iOS and macOS apps — TheFeed expanded to all major platforms while people were still using it under shutdown conditions.
  • Performance optimization — Loading time for a channel went from several minutes in the first version to under 10 seconds.

Less than 1% of the population had access to Telegram during the shutdown. Those who did would download new versions of TheFeed and post them on domestic forums, where the rest of the population could find them. This is how the project spread — organically, through people helping each other.

After GitHub became accessible from inside the country, I added a GitHub relay feature. Users could download updates, configurations, and project news directly from within TheFeed — no separate browser or external tool needed.

5. How It Works

TheFeed operates on a simple insight: even during total internet shutdowns, DNS resolution usually continues to function — because blocking it would break the government's own infrastructure. TheFeed exploits this by encoding content into DNS queries and responses.

Key Technical Properties

  • No tunnel required — Unlike DNSTT and similar tools, TheFeed does not attempt to establish a TCP connection. It pulls content in small fragments through independent DNS queries, making it tolerant of high packet loss and slow resolvers.
  • Two independent content paths — DNS-based feed retrieval and Google-based TeleMirror provide redundancy. If one path is blocked, the other continues working.
  • Three media delivery methods — Inline DNS encoding, DNS relay, and GitHub CDN relay — each independent, each difficult to block without significant collateral damage.
  • Cross-platform — Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, and iOS. Mobile apps use in-process Go via gomobile (no CGo dependency), ensuring a single codebase runs everywhere.
  • Self-updating — Users can download new versions and configurations from within the app itself, without needing an external browser or app store.
  • Cryptographically signed feeds — Content is signed by the server's key, preventing tampering and impersonation.
  • No identifiable traffic patterns — DNS queries look like ordinary domain lookups. There is no persistent connection, no unusual packet sizes, and no detectable protocol fingerprint.

6. After the Shutdown

When the internet was partially restored on May 26, I continued developing TheFeed. The threat of future shutdowns remains, and the tool needed to be ready — not as a wartime prototype, but as a mature, reliable application.

The most significant addition since the shutdown has been an encrypted messenger: a store-and-forward messaging system that works entirely over DNS. If the internet is cut again, people will be able to send short text messages to contacts outside the country — to tell their families they are safe, to coordinate, to stay human.

The messenger did not reach users during the shutdown. But it exists now, ready for the next time.

TheFeed is also applicable beyond a single country. Any population facing an internet shutdown — a pattern seen repeatedly across multiple regions — can deploy and use it.

8. Vision

TheFeed began as an emergency tool. It has grown into something more: a platform for information resilience — one that can serve any population cut off from the open internet.

Near-term goals

  • Harden the encrypted messenger for production readiness
  • Expand public server infrastructure for broader geographic coverage
  • Improve onboarding so non-technical users can set up TheFeed in minutes
  • Build automated health monitoring for public configurations
  • Expand app localization beyond the current English and Farsi UI (documentation already available in English, Farsi, Chinese, and Russian)

Long-term vision

  • Establish TheFeed as a standard tool in the digital-rights toolkit — ready to deploy whenever a shutdown occurs, anywhere in the world
  • Develop a decentralized server network that can be spun up rapidly in response to emerging shutdowns
  • Explore additional low-bandwidth channels beyond DNS (Bluetooth mesh, SMS relay) for environments where even DNS is restricted
  • Build partnerships with digital-rights organizations for pre-positioning infrastructure in at-risk regions

9. About the Developer

TheFeed is built and maintained by a solo developer who experienced the internet shutdown firsthand. The project was born out of personal necessity — not a grant proposal or an academic exercise — and its design reflects the real constraints of operating under severe censorship: unreliable connections, expensive and risky circumvention tools, and the constant possibility of losing access at any moment.

The developer continues to maintain and improve TheFeed full-time alongside a day job, with a commitment to keeping the project open-source and free for all users.
